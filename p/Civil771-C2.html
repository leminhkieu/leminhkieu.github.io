‘mobility’ - is concerned with the movement of through traffic and focused on the efficient movement of people and freight

‘access’ - relates to the ease with which traffic from land abutting roads can enter or leave the road.

Sustainable transport planning: Reduction of transport demand

Modal shift from private to public and active modes of transport (e.g. parking prices, fuel tax, cheap buses)

Reduce the needs for mobility (e.g. through land use planning, working from home scheme)

Shifting the demand in time and space (e.g. congestion charging or cordon congestion toll)

Planning for traffic safety: the Safe System principles

People make mistakes and some crashes are inevitable.

Our bodies don’t withstand crash forces well.

System designers and system users must share responsibility for managing crash forces to a level that doesn’t result in death or serious injury.

We need to strengthen all parts of the system: roads and roadsides, speeds, vehicles and users.

The reasons for high crash rates are many and varied. They include driving behaviour and how the road is engineered but driving speed affects the outcome of every crash.

Equity and inclusion

Equity in transport planning

refers to the fairness with which impacts (benefits and costs) are distributed.

Transportation planning decisions often have significant equity impacts.

Types of equity in transport planning

1. Horizontal Equity

people should be treated the same in the distribution of resources/benefits and costs

2. Vertical Equity With Regard to Income and Social Class

transport policies are equitable if they favor economically and socially disadvantaged groups

3. Vertical Equity With Regard to Mobility Need and Ability

the transportation system meets the needs of travelers with mobility impairments.

"At the same time, the potential benefits of reducing barriers to encourage more everyday physical activity are also much greater. Hence the need to prioritise investment in these communities, and work in partnership."

Trade-offs Between Equity And Other Planning Objectives

Transportation planning often involves tradeoffs between equity objectives and other planning objectives.

For example, improving pedestrian safety may reduce traffic speeds and therefore economic productivity.

There is no standard way to determine how much weight to give a particular equity objective; such planning decisions should reflect community needs and values.
