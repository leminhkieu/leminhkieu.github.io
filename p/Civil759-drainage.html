Highway Surface Drainage

To design a structure with the proper capacity to divert or remove water from the roadway and pass collected water under the roadway.

Proper roadway drainage is imperative for safety and traffic flow during storm events

The design of a hydraulic structure requires knowing how much water is associated with the design storm (hydrology) and calculating the velocity, depth, and type of flow (hydraulics) that must be accounted for in the design
 
 
 
 
 

Learning outcomes:

6. Apply and integrate fundamental knowledge of hydrology and hydraulics, road geometry and surfacing properties to calculate transport surface drainage requirements.

Assessment: Small quiz, no project, approximately 30% of the Final Exam, mostly numerical problems

Road Drainage can be conveniently divided into those relating to the flow of:

Surface Water – concerned with the measures taken to control the movement of water over the ground on and adjacent to the highway; and

Subsurface Water – concerned with the flow of water within soils.

We focus on drainage of Surface water on road surface in this paper

Run-off

Ultimately the highway engineer is concerned with the maximum rate of surface run-off from the catchment area.

However, the maximum rate is dependent on a great number of conditions, is never constant over a given catchment area and varies considerably during a single storm.

So how do we arrive at any one design figure which will take all the previous considerations into account?

In reality we compromise between adequately meeting the most severe design requirements and still being within the economic scope of the scheme.

Rational Method

One of the most commonly used procedures for calculating peak flows from small drainages of less than 200 acres is the Rational Method.

The Rational Method has been in use in some form or another at least since 1889. It is not so much that the method itself is judged as being rational, but more than the method relates to the ratio between rainfall and runoff (which is essentially the runoff coefficient, $C$).

For catchments larger than the areas above, the Rational Method tends to give a conservative result. Therefore for larger areas a dynamic analysis by computer modelling using a profiled hyetograph should be used

We focus on the Rational Method in this course

Rational Method

\[\begin{aligned} Q = 0.278 \times C \times I \times A \\ \end{aligned} \]

\[\begin{aligned} Q = 2.78 \times C \times I \times A \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where: $Q$ = peak flow/discharge in $m^3/sec$ (l/s)

$C$ = Peak runoff coefficient (dimensionless)

$I$ = average rainfall intensity over the whole catchment in $mm/hr$ for a duration corresponding to the time of concentration

$A$ = catchment area in $km^2$ (hectares)

Note:

The unit and formula in Blue colour are used together

0.278 (2.78) is a conversion from one to another units

1 $km^2$ = 100 hectares ; 1 $m^3/sec$ = 1000 l/s

Basic Assumptions about Rational Method:

Drainage areas are smaller than 300 acres.

For larger catchment areas the precision is somewhat limited and generally tends to overestimate maximum runoff due to assumptions made in the model.

Peak flow occurs when the entire catchment is contributing, or in other words, at time of concentration $T_c$.

Rainfall intensity is uniform over a duration of time equal to or greater than the time of concentration $T_c$ .

Time of Concentration $T_c$ = Duration of rainfall

$T_c$ is defined as the time required for the surface runoff from the remotest part of the drainage basin to reach the point under consideration. Or in other words, time required for an entire watershed to contribute to runoff at the point of interest for hydraulic design

Note that $T_c$ does not solely depend on the distance to the point of consideration

The method is best suited to urban areas or relatively small catchments in rural locations....and well suited to highway surface drainage design.

The assumption of a uniform rainfall intensity over the entire catchment is valid because of the small catchment area.

The value of $Q$, as the formular below suggests, depends on the peak runoff coefficient $C$ and average rainfall intensity $I$

$C$ are essentially constant as the runoff is from buildings and roads. Also the difficulty in assessing $C$ is solved as the runoff is from buildings and roads, hence $C ≈ 1$ (impermeable).

\[\begin{aligned} Q = 0.278 \times C \times I \times A \\ \end{aligned} \]

How do we have $C$ and $I$?

Values of Impermeability Factors or Runoff Coefficient $C$

$C$ represents the ratio of runoff to rainfall.

\[\begin{aligned} Q = 0.278 \times C \times I \times A \\ \end{aligned} \]

$C$ is the percentage of rain water that is available in the form of runoff

If the ground is relatively impervious, more runoff takes place, $C$ will be larger and closer to 1

Rational Method: Values of peak runoff coefficient $C$

Impermeability Factors / Runoff Coefficient

How to estimate $C$ when there are multiple areas?

Let $A_1, A_2,$ ..., $A_n$ = areas of the different surface on the catchment area.

$C_1, C_2,$ ..., $C_n$ = corresponding runoff coefficient for different surfaces

Average peak runoff coefficient =

\[\begin{aligned} C_{avg} = \frac{C_1A_1 + C_2A_2+...+C_nA_n}{A_1+A_2+...+A_n} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Rational Method

\[\begin{aligned} Q = 0.278 \times C \times I \times A \\ \end{aligned} \]

Frequency / Return Period / Average Recurrence Interval (ARI)

The chosen Average Recurrence Interval (ARI) is an economic decision. Typically a 1 in 2 or 1 in 5 year return period is chosen. However, for a motorway a 1 in 10 return period might be justifiable.

So far, in order to estimate the rainfal intensity $I$ in the IDF curves, we are always given both Duration (or Time of concentration) and Frequency (Return period or ARI)

In practice, we often don't know the Duration and Frequency of future storms at the design phase of a highway project

Design Duration

If we don't know the Time of concentration, we'll need to 'design' or assume one

From a theoretical perspective, it is assumed that rainfall Duration exactly equals catchment time of concentration $T_c$.

The rainfall intensity is then can be estimated using that assumed Time of concentration, using the IDF curve

So, how do we choose a Time of concentration ?

Various graphical and formula based methods are available to estimate $T_c$ being estimated using hydraulic equations of flow. For example:

Kirpich Formula

Kerby’s Equation

Friend’s Equation

NZTA Equation

Kirpich formula for $T_c$: Suitable for rural areas

\[\begin{aligned} T_c = \big( \frac{0.87 \times L^2}{1000 \times S} \big) ^ {0.385} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$0.87$ = calibrating constant

$T_c$ = time of concentration in hours

$L$ = length of flow (km)

$S$ = average slope (m/m)

Kerby’s Equation: Kerby (1959)

\[\begin{aligned} T_c = c(L \times n \times s^{-0.5})^{0.467} \\ \end{aligned} \]

for $ L \leq 365$ and where:

$T_c$ = time of concentration in hours

$L$ = length of flow (meters)

$n$ = retardance roughness coefficient (smooth pavements = 0.02, poor grass, bare sod = 0.30, average grass = 0.40, dense grass = 0.80)

$s$ = Slope (m/m)

$c$ = 1.44 (when using meters)

Friend’s Equation: suitable for urban and rural residential areas

\[\begin{aligned} T_c = \frac{107 \times n \times L^{0.333}}{S^{0.2}} \\ \end{aligned} \]

where:

$T_c$ = overland sheet flow travel time (minutes)

$L$ = overland sheet path length (meters)

$n$ = Horton’s roughness value for the surface

Asphalt/concrete: 0.010 – 0.012, Bare sand: 0.010 - 0.060, Bare clay/loam: 0.012 - 0.033, Gravelled surface: 0.012 – 0.030, Short grass: 0.100 – 0.200, Lawns: 0.200 – 0.300, Pasture: 0.300 – 0.400, Dense shrubbery: 0.400

$S$ = Slope (%)

Read more: https://www.ccc.govt.nz/assets/Documents/Environment/Water/waterways-guide/21.RainfallAndRunoff.pdf

NZTA Equations: for time of flow over a pavement surface in seconds

\[\begin{aligned} T_c = \frac{500 \times n \times l_f^{(2/3)}}{S_f^{(1/3)}} \\ \end{aligned} \]

where:

$l_f$ = Flow path length (m)

$n$ = Manning's roughness coefficient (quite similar to Horton's but not exactly identical)

https://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/mannings-roughness-d_799.html

$S_f$ = Flow path slope (m/m)

Planning for Aquaplanning: How to estimate and deal with it?

Wet Road Surface vs Conditions for Aquaplaning

Technically, a wet road surface occurs when precipitation intensity exceeds 0.1mm/hr, we are interested in much higher rainfall intensities (up to 50 mm/hr).

Aquaplaning occurs when vehicle's speed increases, resulting in decreased contact time, possibly to a state where there is not enough time to remove the water and make contact with the road surface.

Governing Factors for Aquaplaning

What are the factors which influence the occurrence of aquaplaning?

Aquaplaning: Road Texture

Although not widely accepted around the world the road surface should also have a degree of texture.

There are multiple technologies to improve road texture, read more: https://www.fhwa.dot.gov/publications/research/safety/14065/004.cfm

What do you see?

Macrotexture is measured by:

Sand patch (SP) test (View more here: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OmYfi6JaYgw)

Sensor measured texture depth (SMTD)

 

It's important to calculate the water film depths to avoid aquaplanning

NZTA have adopted the NAASRA (1974) equation to calculate water film depths.

\[\begin{aligned} d = 0.046 \frac{(l_f \times I )^{0.5}}{S_f^{0.2}} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$d$ = Depth of flow (mm) at the end of flow path

$l_f$ = Flow path length (m)

$I$ = Rainfall intensity (mm/hr)

$S_f$ = Flow path slope (m/m)

Flow path slope is given by: $S_f = (S_l^2 + S_c^2)^{0.5}$

Where:

$S_l$ = Longitudinal slope (%)

$S_c$ = Cross fall of pavement (%)

Flow path length is given by: $l_f = W \frac{S_f}{S_c}$

Where:

$W$ = Width of pavement contributing to flow (m)

NZTA Design Criteria

Critical depth for aquaplaning ranges from 4mm to 10mm. Surface water depth should therefore be restricted to 4mm

Two year return period is sufficient

Minimum time of concentration of 5 min should be used

FHWA Equation

The Federal Highway Administration in the US suggest the following equation (converted to SI units) to calculate water film depths.

\[\begin{aligned} D = 0.103 \frac{T^{0.11} \times L^{0.43} \times I^{0.59}}{S^{0.42}} - T \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$D$ = Water film depth above top of pavement texture (mm)

$T$ = Average pavement texture depth (mm)

$I$ = Rainfall intensity (mm/h)

$L$ = Length of flow path (m) -- similar to $l_f$ in NZTA equation

$S$ = Slope of flow path (%) -- similar to $S_f$ in NZTA equation but different unit

Estimation of drainage path

The designer needs to determine the drainage path length by plotting and assessing the contours on the proposed road surface.

The drainage path is the route taken by rainfall runoff from the point at which it falls on the carriageway to the carriageway edge

The water depth analysis shall be carried out on the critical drainage path, i.e. the longest drainage path on the carriageway within the assessment location.

In many cases, the equation $l_f = W \frac{S_f}{S_c}$ may be used for simplicity

 

Design criteria specifying maximum water film depth should be adhered to.

Design Speed less than 80km/h

Water film depth is no greater than 5mm$^{*}$

Pavement texture is 0.2mm minimum

Design Speed of 80-100km/h or higher

Check for rainfall intensity 50mm/h$^{**}$

Water film depth is 2.5mm desirable, 4mm maximum$^{*}$

Pavement texture is 0.4mm desirable minimum

Pavement crossfall 2% to 4%

$^{*}$ We can calculate this water film depth using the FHWA and NZTA equations above.

$^{**}$ 50 mm/hr results in reduced visibility hence drivers slow down with some drivers starting to reduce speed as low as 25 km/hr. Higher intensities are therefore not practical for design purposes.

Calculate the required inlet spacing:

\[\begin{aligned} Q = q \times L \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$L$ = required inlet spacing, m

$q$ = peak runoff (m$^3$/sec)

$Q$ = discharge into inlet (m$^3$/sec) or channel capacity (at inlet)

\[\begin{aligned} L = Q / q \\ \end{aligned} \]

How do we calcualte pavement peak runoff $q$ ?

Pavement runoff is calculated by the Rational method, however, the contribution to the flow at the kerb is given by a modification to the formula as follows:

\[\begin{aligned} q = \frac{C \times I \times W}{3.6 \times 10^6} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$q$ = peak run-off contribution per longitudinal metre of pavement, m$^3$/sec

$C$ = coefficient of runoff (e.g. equals 1 for impermeable)

$I$ = rainfall intensity, mm/hr

$W$ = width of contributing cross section, m

How do we calculate the discharge into inlet $Q$ (m$^3$/sec) or channel capacity (at inlet): NZTA Equation

\[\begin{aligned} Q = \frac{0.375 \times S_L^{0.5} \times d^{8/3}}{n \times S_c} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$Q$ = discharge into inlet (m$^3$/sec)

$S_c$ = channel crossfall (m/m)

$S_L$ = longitudinal channel slope (m/m)

$d$ = depth of flow at kerb face (m)

$n$ = Manning’s roughness coefficient

In the Rational method, note that the value of rainfaill intensity $I$ needs to be found from the IDF curve, thus therefore requires the values of Frequency/ARI and Time of concentration of the storm

We often apply the NZTA minimum time of concentration: $T_c$ = 5 min

With the calculated discharge into inlet $Q$, we can actually calculate $T_c$ to see if it's actually less than 5 min or not, if $T_c \leq 5$ min our assumption is valid

$T_c$ can be found by suming values of flow time over the various parts of the catchment, assume no over flow

$T_c = T_E + T_G + T_P$

where:

$T_E$ = Time of entry to inlet, if water is flowing on the surface, we'll have $T_E=T_s$, otherwise time of entry should be given to us

$T_s$ = Time of flow over a pavement surface

$T_G$ = Gutter flow time

$T_P$ = Pipe flow time

Read more: https://www.nzta.govt.nz/assets/resources/highway-surface-drainage-design-guide/docs/highway-surface-drainage-design-guide.pdf

NZTA equation: Time of flow over a pavement surface in seconds is given by:

\[\begin{aligned} T_s =500 \times n \times \frac{l_f^{2/3}}{ S_f^{1/3}} \\ \end{aligned} \]

where:

$n$ = Mannings roughness coefficient

$l_f$ = Flow path length (m)

$S_f$ = Flow path slope (m/m)

Remind that flow path slope is given by: $S_f = (S_l^2 + S_c^2)^{0.5}$

Where:

$S_l$ = Longitudinal slope (%)

$S_c$ = Cross fall of pavement (%)

Flow path length is given by: $l_f = W \frac{S_f}{S_c}$

Where:

$W$ = Width of pavement contributing to flow (m)

Gutter flow time is given by:

\[\begin{aligned} T_G = \frac{5 \times L}{ 4 \times v_I} \\ \end{aligned} \]

where:

$v_I$ = water velocity in the gutter

$L$ = length of contributing gutter (m)

Pipe flow time is given by:

\[\begin{aligned} T_P = \frac{L_p}{ v_p} \\ \end{aligned} \]

where:

$L_p$ = length of contributing pipe (m)

$v_p$ = Water velocity in the pipe

The water velocity is calculated by:

\[\begin{aligned} v = Q / A \\ \end{aligned} \]

where:

$Q$ = discharge into gutter or pipe (m$^3$/sec)

$A$ = Total cross area of gutter of pipe (m$^2$)

Highway Surface Drainage Channels

 
 
 

Open Channel Flow

Flow in an open channel takes place not because of a pressure difference between two points but because of a difference in elevation between two points.

We often need to determine whether the side channel is capable of handling the estimated discharge without the water eroding the sides or entering the pavement.

Most open channel hydraulic formulae in use today are based on the work of Antoine Léonard de Chézy, a French Engineer and mathematician.

Chézy Formula

The basic hydraulic formulas for determining flow of water in open channels are as follows:

\[\begin{aligned} V = C \sqrt{R \times S} \\ Q = A \times V \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$Q$ = discharge ($m^3/s$)

$C$ = coefficient of roughness

$R$ = $A/P$ = average hydraulic radius ($m$)

$S$ = slope of hydraulic gradient ($m/m$)

$A$ = cross-sectional area of flow ($m^2$)

$P$ = wetted perimeter of the flow ($m$)

$V$ = average velocity of water ($m/s$)

Manning Formula

The most widely used in highway drainage design is Manning’s Formula, applies for full flow not under pressure. This formula gives the value for C in the Chézy formula as:

\[\begin{aligned} C = \frac{R^{1/6}}{n} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$C$ = coefficient of roughness

$n$ = Manning’s roughness coefficient

$R$ = $A/P$ = hydraulic radius (m)

$A$ = cross-sectional area of flow ($m^2$)

$P$ = wetted perimeter of the flow ($m$)

Replace $C$ in

\[\begin{aligned} V = C \sqrt{R \times S} \\ \end{aligned} \]

We then have the Simplified Manning Formula

\[\begin{aligned} V = \frac{R^{2/3} \times S^{1/2}}{n} \\ \end{aligned} \]

And

\[\begin{aligned} Q= A \times V = \frac{A \times R^{2/3} \times S^{1/2}}{n} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$n$ = Manning’s roughness coefficient

$R$ = A/P = average hydraulic radius (m)

$A$ = cross-sectional area of flow ($m^2$)

$P$ = wetted perimeter of the flow ($m$)

$S$ = slope of hydraulic gradient ($m/m$)

 
 

Critical Velocity

Manning’s formula assumes steady state conditions and a uniform flow. For more complex flow conditions refer to your hydraulics lecture notes.

Manning’s formula does, however, provide a reasonable estimate for calculating the water velocity (and hence potential for scour) in the ditch.

The final flow velocity should be kept below the safe flow velocity, $V_{max}$. Where $V_{max}$ can be found in the previous tables. Hence:

\[\begin{aligned} V = \frac{R^{2/3} \times S^{1/2}}{n} \leq V_{max} \\ \end{aligned} \]
