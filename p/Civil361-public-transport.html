We focus on On-road public transport in this course. It involves the provisions for mainly buses, light rail and mobility-as-a-service. The physical components of a system can be classified as:

Vehicles;

Right-of-way (ROW);

Passenger facilities.

Right of way (ROW) is the strip of land on which the PT vehicles operate. There are three board categories, distinguished by the degree of separation from other traffic:

Category (A) Dedicated: without grade crossing or any legal access by other vehicles or pedestrians. Includes tunnels, overhead tracks and with over-ride at crossing points. (e.g. Northern Busway)

Category (B) Dual Mode: physical separation by curbs, barriers or grade separation from other traffic. However, there is at-grade crossing points for other vehicles and pedestrians (e.g. bus lanes with curbs)

Category (C) Shared: where PT services share the road with other vehicles (e.g. buses on arterial roads)

In general traffic engineers will be engaged in the design and management of Category B and C ROW – why??

Modern public transport modes:

Bus Rapid Transit (BRT)

Light Rail Transit (LRT)

 
 

Light Rail Transit (LRT) employs a fully segregated and often grade-separated ROW, advanced control systems, but light (hence the name) trains. Always ROW category A and B.

They are often seen as intermediate between bus and metro systems in terms of both capacity and cost. Everything a BRT system can do, can be performed by a light rail transit (LRT) system in terms of capacity and service levels. The infrastructure cost is more compared to BRT. However, a single driver is required for several units (higher capacity). There is also greater attraction to passengers due to vehicle design and image.

Actual capacity

Bus: 70 pass/bus, 15-min headway = 280 pass/hr

BRT: 200 pass/bus, 10-min headway = 1200 pass/hr

LRT: 2-car trains, 150 pass/car, 5-min headway = 6000 pass/hr

Rail: 10-car trains, 200 pass/car, 5-min headway = 40,000 pass/hr

Transit Oriented Development (TOD)

TOD implies high quality, thoughtful planning and design of land use and infrastructure to support, facilitate and prioritise not only the use of transit, but the most basic modes of transport, walking and cycling.

Focuses on developing communities that push away from car-centric city forms, towards an efficient walking, cycling and transit city.

Read more: https://www.itdp.org/2017/06/23/tod-standard/

Future of mobility: https://stfalcon.com/en/blog/post/mobility-as-a-service

Mobility as a Service (MaaS) is the concept of shifting transportation solutions to an on-demand service. Maas is the integration of various forms of transport services into a single mobility service accessible on demand.

Instead of individuals owning and operating their own vehicles, MaaS providers offer transport options when and where the user requires them. In much the same way that Netflix and Spotify provide convenient access to TV, movies, and music.

To meet a customer’s request, a MaaS operator facilitates a diverse menu of transport options, be they public transport, ride-, car- or bike-sharing, taxi or car rental/lease, or a combination thereof.

 
 

Challenges in Maas:

1. Multi-modal routing for a large number of travellers

2. Service coordination and coverage

3. Data transfer and operation in real-time

4. Policy dilemma: who should control Maas?
