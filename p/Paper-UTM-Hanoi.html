Perception index

We define a ‘Perception index’ as a measure of individual perception on 7 different major risks to individuals: Crime, Water, Food, Work accident, Mental health, Traffic and Serious weather events

\[\begin{aligned} P^k_c = \frac{\sum{p_{i,c}^k}}{N_c} \\ \end{aligned} \]

For each risk, we give a value of 1 if the respondent is “very worried", 0.5 if they are “somewhat worried" and 0 if they are “not worried" towards the risk.

Experience index

We define an `Experience index' from the data to measure the frequency or intensity of individual encounters with the risk.

\[\begin{aligned} E^k_c = \frac{\sum{e_{i,c}^k}}{N_c}\\ \end{aligned} \]

Value of $e_{i,c}^k$ is either 0 (no harm), 0.25 (know someone who experienced harm), 0.5 (experienced harm) or 1 (both).

A new resilience index

The Work Risk Poll survey dataset has several questions related to personal resilience:

  • Suppose you lost all of your household income, how long would you be able to cover your basic needs?
  • Are your neighbours care about you and your well-being?
  • Is your national government well prepared to deal with a disaster?
  • Are your hospitals well prepared to deal with a disaster?
  • Are you and your family well prepared to deal with a disaster?
  • Is your local government well prepared to deal with a disaster?
  • Could you protect yourself and your family in a future disasters?
  • Does the plan for future disaster known by all household member in your family?

A new resilience index

We encode the answers for these questions with a value between 0 to 1. We assign 1 to the most resilient answer (e.g. “Yes, well prepared"), 0 to the least (e.g. “No, not well prepared") and 0.5 to the middle/unsure answer (e.g. “It depends"). A country-wide resilience index could then be defined as follows:

\[\begin{aligned} R_c = \frac{\sum{\sum{r_{i,c}^m}}}{N_c \times N_m}\\ \end{aligned} \]

Conclusion

Overall, individual Perception, Experience and Resilience to risks and harms are closely linked. Improving Resilience requires a comprehensive approach involving multiple aspects of personal life

People in developed countries experience a lot less harms compared to people to people in developing countries, so as a result they also perceive risks as a lot less severe.

When looking at individual risks, traffic and then crime are the main concerns for people

There is in general a linear relationship between experience and perception of risks, but when we look into this more closely we can find some interesting facts about how people experience harms and how they perceive them

A new resilience index enables us to classify countries into 4 main groups: High-risk vulnerable, Low-risk vulnerable, Low-risk resilient, and Disaster resilient.

The relationship between the Resilience and Experience index show that countries are different in terms of their adaptation to risks, with some adapting much better than others

Generally, there is also a week linear relationship between individual perception and their resilience to risks, as when people perceive the risks as less severe they also tend to be more resilience to them
