Welcome to CIVIL 303

Almost everything you do today involved the transport network.

Over the coming weeks you will learn how that system is planned, designed, operated, and renewed and how engineers like you keep it working for 5.3 million New Zealanders.

What to expect today
A tour of the course, the industry that hires its graduates, and the real-world problems you will soon be solving.

What is transport engineering?

A branch of civil engineering focused on the planning, design, operation, and maintenance of transport systems for all users: drivers, freight, public transport passengers, cyclists, and pedestrians.

In practice, the work falls into four overlapping streams. Most transport engineers move between them across a career.

1

Traffic engineering

Classic detailed-design work: checking car parks and loading docks against standards, signal timing, geometric design. Traffic flow theory in action.

2

Transport modelling

Using software like Aimsun, VISSIM, and EMME to predict how networks will behave and to stress-test designs before they're built.

3

Transport planning

High-level strategy: where to put cycle lanes, how to improve PT access, how to align with council strategies. Less calculation, more report-writing and policy framing.

4

Pavements

Designing and renewing the physical road structure — materials, layers, drainage, surfacing. Engineering that has to last decades under heavy loads.

CIVIL 303 covers all four streams
Topics 1–2 are on OPERATING a transport system, Topic 3 is long-term STRATEGIC PLANNING, and Topic 4 is BUILD & RENEW of our pavements. By the end of semester you'll know which stream fits you best.

The reality of being a transport engineer

✓ The pros

Job stability
Population growth and urban congestion mean demand for better traffic solutions only goes up. Transport engineers are rarely out of work.
A dynamic field
Intelligent transport systems, Autonomous vehicles, Electrification, Smart highways.
Creative latitude
At the planning end you have real flexibility. Engineering judgement carries more weight than in some other civil disciplines.

✗ The cons

Public scrutiny
Traffic is personal. Every project draws attention and everyone has their own views.
Communication-heavy
Most projects involve multiple agencies with competing agendas. Negotiation and writing skills matter as much as the maths.
Pay ceiling
Australasian benchmarks put transport engineer pay at roughly $81–$128k. This is solid, but slightly below geotechnical or structure specialisations at the top of the table.

1. Traffic measurements & studies

You can't manage what you can't measure. Before designing anything, engineers characterise the flow: how many vehicles, when, how fast, in what direction, in what mix.

You'll learn to:

  • Measure flow, speed, density, headway, gap
  • Design and conduct traffic and turning movement studies
  • Interpret count data, peak-hour factors, AADT
  • Choose the right method: manual, loop, video, Bluetooth, probe data
Industry context
NZTA's national open-data dashboard (covering ~11,000 km of state highway and ~83,000 km of local roads) is built from exactly these measurements. Knowing how the data was generated is what lets you trust or challenge what it says.
Topic 2 · Modelling and simulation

5. Modelling & simulation

Macro models

Whole-network strategic models — Auckland Regional Transport Model, Wellington Transport Strategic Model. Tools: EMME, VISUM.

Micro simulation

Individual vehicle trajectories on time-space diagrams. Tools: VISSIM, AIMSUN, SUMO. Used to design intersections, signal timings, motorway merges.

Core relationships

  • Speed–flow–density fundamental diagram
  • Traffic flow models (Greenshields)
  • Capacity of un-signalised intersections (gap-acceptance theory)
  • Queueing theory at bottlenecks
Topic 6 · Operational analysis

6. Operational analysis of transport facilities

Every road, intersection, bus stop, and station has a capacity and an associated Level of Service (LOS) — a letter grade from A (free-flowing) to F (gridlock) that captures how well it's performing.

A → F
The LOS scale you'll use on every analysis
2,200
veh/h/lane — the canonical capacity of a freeway lane (HCM)
200,000
vehicles a day on SH1 in central Auckland — NZ's busiest road

You'll learn the Highway Capacity Manual (HCM) framework as adapted for NZ practice, and apply it to multi-lane highways, signalised and un-signalised intersections.

Topic 2 · Transport and land use

2. Transport & land use

Transport networks shape where people live and work; land use shapes how, when, and how far they travel. The two cannot be designed separately.

You'll explore:

  • The feedback loop between accessibility and development
  • Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) — concentrating density around stations
  • How induced demand undermines "just build more lanes"
  • Auckland's Unitary Plan as a live example
Live case study
The new City Rail Link stations at Maungawhau, Karangahape, and Te Waihorotiu will trigger dense redevelopment within walking distance. Designing the transport without designing the land use — or vice versa — wastes billions.
Topic 3 · Transportation planning

3. Transportation planning

The classic four-step model is still the workhorse of strategic transport planning in New Zealand. Every business case for a major project rests on it.

1

Trip generation

How many trips originate in each zone?

2

Trip distribution

Where do they go? (Gravity model)

3

Mode choice

Car, bus, train, walk, bike?

4

Assignment

Which route do they take?

Why it matters in NZ
Every dollar of the $32.9B NLTP is justified using a business case built on these four steps. Knowing where the assumptions live is what makes you a useful engineer rather than a calculator.
Topic 4 · Active modes and equity

4. Active modes & equity

The cheapest, healthiest, lowest-carbon trip is the one taken on foot or by bike. Designing for these modes isn't optional — it's central.

You'll learn to:

  • Design pedestrian crossings, footpaths, kerb extensions
  • Design protected and on-road cycle facilities
  • Apply transport equity — who benefits, who pays, who's excluded
  • Recognise hypermobility and its effect on mode choice
Policy context
The 2024–27 NLTP halved walking and cycling funding (from $910m to $460m). The future of active-mode infrastructure depends on engineers making the case rigorously — with data — not on advocacy alone.
Source: NZ Herald, "National Land Transport Programme shake-up", Sept 2024.

7. Pavement design & rehabilitation

The big picture · How it all fits together

How the seven topics connect

A real project — say, a new corridor between Drury and Pukekohe — uses every topic in this course, in roughly this order:

1 Land-use forecasts feed 2 demand modelling, which sizes the corridor.

3 Mode choice tells you how much goes to bus, train, car, bike.

4 Active-mode and equity analysis tells you who the corridor must serve.

5 Micro-simulation tests intersection and merge performance.

6 Operational analysis sets the number of lanes and the LOS target.

7 Pavement design turns the geometry into a structure that lasts 25–40 years.

The integration is the point
A common failure mode in industry is treating these as separate silos. CIVIL 303 deliberately teaches them as one workflow — which is what graduates tell us made them effective on day one.

Practicalities · How to succeed

How to make the most of CIVIL 303

Show up curious

Every concept here is alive in the city you live in. Notice the cycle lane, the chipseal repair, the queue at the lights — and ask why.

Practice the maths

Capacity, gravity models, mechanistic design — these are quantitative. Doing the problems is non-negotiable.

Read what industry reads

NZTA's research reports, Austroads guides, and the NLTP factsheets are short, free, and exactly what your future employer reads.

Your number-one tip
Form a study group early. Transport engineering is collaborative in practice — and the people sitting near you will be your professional network for the next 40 years.
