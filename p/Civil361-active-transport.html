Planning for traffic safety: the Safe System principles

People make mistakes and some crashes are inevitable.

Our bodies don’t withstand crash forces well.

System designers and system users must share responsibility for managing crash forces to a level that doesn’t result in death or serious injury.

We need to strengthen all parts of the system: roads and roadsides, speeds, vehicles and users.

The reasons for high crash rates are many and varied. They include driving behaviour and how the road is engineered but driving speed affects the outcome of every crash.

Design process for pedestrian facilities

Step 1: Determine the demand

Step 2: A strategy which incorporates all aspects of pedestrian comfort, convenience and safety needs to be developed.

Safety: analysis of the number of pedestrians injured. What were the common factors? (severity, frequency, age group)

Amenity & convenience: people with disability, infrastructure quality (determined by standards)

Delay: pedestrian delay or evidence of pedestrian impatience.

Step 3: Within the established road hierarchy (the classification of the road), and road user hierarchy (types of road users), what scope is allowed for the implementation of treatments?

Is it a simple solution? Low-key measurements such as improved street lighting, parking restrictions, repairs etc. should be prioritised

Is a more advance solution required? Further analysis is required to determine whether the treatment needs to be at the site or whether the issue is some distance away.

Pedestrian environment concept 1: Sharing the main street

The main streets of rural towns, and minor arterial roads in cities that are straddled by strips of retail, commercial and community activities, have conflicting traffic and pedestrian needs that need to be managed. Pedestrian crashes cluster at such locations.

Sharing the main street means adapting it – or a centre along a minor arterial road – to improve the safety and the quality of the road environment for all its users.

Example: Parnell Rd, Auckland

Read more: Sharing the main street (Westerman, 1993)

Pedestrian environment concept 2: Living streets

The concept of ‘living streets’ recognises that, as a priority, streets should be designed with living and community interaction. While cars are not excluded, they are designed so drivers are aware they are in an area where pedestrian and other users are important

Example: High Street, Auckland

Read more: Mannual for the Streets (UK)

Pedestrian environment concept 3: Shared zones

A shared zone is a residential or retail street that has been designed to give priority to residents and pedestrians while significantly reducing the dominance of motorised vehicles

Motorised vehicles still have access but must give way to pedestrians; and conversely pedestrians should not hinder vehicles. The route is physically constrained for vehicles by landscaping, structures and tight turning radii, with no delineation between the footpath and roadway.

Example: Fort Street, Auckland

Read more: Pedestrian facilities users’ guide (FHWA-US)

Pedestrian environment concept 4: Pedestrian precincts

Most pedestrian-only areas are created by restricting traffic access or closing roads to traffic. Four types:

1. Modified street precinct: one block is closed for pedestrian-only use.

2. Plaza: several blocks are closed but the cross-streets stay open to all traffic.

3. Continuous: several blocks and the cross-streets are closed.

4. Displaced: walkways are developed away from the usual roadside footpaths, making use of lanes and alleys.

Example: High Street trial, Auckland

Read more: Design and safety of pedestrian facilities – A recommended practice of the Institute of Transportation Engineers (US)

The cycle network plan

A bicycle network is made up of a series of bicycle routes connected together to link many origins to many destinations. Thereby, an effective network has no or small gaps which links routes seamlessly.

There are two main types of network – utility (or functional) network and recreational network.

Utility network: Connects destinations for functional trip purposes, including shopping, work, education etc. Connections should be as direct as possible as utility cyclists want to get from A to B as quickly as possible.

Recreational network: Recreational routes can pass through urban areas/centres (and therefore also form part of the utility network), but the focus is on leisure cycling. They can include signed long-distance routes, signed tourist themed routes or a collection of nodes and interconnected links, enabling cyclists to determine their own trip. Recreational cyclists are typically looking for a leisurely and attractive ride, which can allow them to explore an area, exercise or socialise.

Creating cycle facilities

The primary goal of designing and constructing bicycle networks is to create high-quality space in which cyclist can ride.

Method 1: Reduce the width of parking and/or traffic lanes. This is a low cost method.

Method 2: Seal shoulders: Gravel shoulders can be sealed to provide cycle lanes.

Method 3: Indent car parking: This removes parked cars from through lanes and thus reduces cyclist from being “squished” by parked cars and traffic.

Method 4: Prohibiting car parking: Use the lanes for cyclists.

Creating cycle facilities

Method 5: Widen the road into the median: Reducing the width of medians can provide space for cycle lanes. However, it must be ensured that medians are wide enough to accommodate turning lanes.

Method 6: Remove traffic lane: Some roads may have too many lanes for its traffic flow.

Method 7: High standard off-road paths: Depending on the volume of cyclists, consideration should be given to a high-standard off-road path that provides equivalent connectivity, continuity and service.

To determine the most appropriate method, the costs and benefits to society needs to be weighed.

Exercise: Pick a road in Auckland and see how can we introduce cycling facilities on it

The most important aspects can be considered to be safety, directness and cohesion.

Safety: Avoiding conflict with crossing traffic, separating different types of road users where necessary due to traffic volumes/speeds, reducing speed at points of conflict, ensuring good visibility of cyclists, and ensuring that there are recognisable road categories.

Directness: distance or time required to cycle between the origin and destination. In urban areas, it is desirable for cycling to be quicker than driving, which can be achieved through providing more direct routes for bicycles rather than for cars. To maximise directness there should also be minimal detours, good priority for bicycles, and the ability for cyclists to maintain constant speeds. The directness of a route in distance can be determined by a detour factor.

Cohesion: ensures clear wayfinding and minimal interruption. There should be a consistent high quality of provision across the network and all common trip origins and destinations within a city should be accessible by bike and connected to the network. Intermodal cohesion is also desirable, enabling and encouraging cycling trips as a means of transport to and from public transport nodes. Without cohesion there cannot be a cycle network, only a collection of single cycle routes.
