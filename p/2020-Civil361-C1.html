What is 'traffic flow'?

The total number of vehicles passing a given point in a given time. Traffic flow is expressed as vehicles per hour (veh/hr)

\[\begin{aligned} Flow(q) \quad q = \frac{N}{T} \ (veh/hr) \\ \end{aligned} \]

Traffic flow data can be collected by:

manual counting with human surveyors

road tubes

loop detectors

big data e.g. mobile phone data, etc

Traffic volume studies

How to quantify the number of vehicles passing a point or roadway during a given time interval?

Volume – the total number of vehicles that pass over a given point or section during an extended period (beyond an hour) and usually 24 hours.

Peak Hourly Volume (PHV) - PHV is the maximum flow observed over any 1 hour period. In urban areas where tidal flows are common, it is usual to have two PHV’s, one for the A.M. and other for P.M. In rural areas it is sufficient to use only one PHV.

Traffic volume studies

Annual Average Daily Traffic (AADT): is the average number of vehicles per day on a road over the year.

Average Daily Traffic (ADT) refers to the average number of vehicles on a road during a 7 day week (or 5 day working week).

Peak Hour Factor (PHF)

Compares the traffic volume during the busiest 15-minutes of the peak hour with the total volume during the peak hour. It indicates how consistent traffic volume is during the peak hour.

\[\begin{aligned} PHF = \frac{V}{V_{15m} \times 4} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Where:

$V$ = Total hourly volume

$V_{15m}$ = volumne during the peak 15 minutes of the analysis hour

When PHF is known, a peak flow rate for a peak 15 minute within the analysis hour can be calculated:

\[\begin{aligned} v = \frac{V}{PHF} \\ \end{aligned} \]

What is a 'Time-Space Diagram'

Shows the position of individual vehicles in time and in space

Useful for understanding traffic flow. These diagrams are especially useful for discussions of shock waves and wave propagation

Typically, time is drawn on the horizontal axis and distance from a reference point on the vertical axis. The trajectories of individual vehicles in motion are portrayed in this diagram by sloping lines, and stationary vehicles are represented by horizontal lines. The slope of the line represents the speed of the vehicle. Curved portions of the trajectories represent vehicles undergoing speed changes such as deceleration.

Use space-mean speeds when reducing and analyzing travel time data.

There are two ways to measure mean speed:

Time-mean-speed is calculated from the individual spot speed recorded for vehicles passing a point over a selected time period

\[\begin{aligned} v_t = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N{v_i}}{N} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N{\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t_i}}}{N} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Space-mean-speed is calculated by dividing the average travel time by the measured distance

\[\begin{aligned} v_s = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta \bar{t}} = \frac{\Delta x}{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N{\frac{\Delta x} {v_i}}} = \frac{N}{\sum_{i=1}^N{\frac{1} {v_i}}} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Example 2: If the spot speeds are 50, 40, 30, 20 and 10 km/h, find the time mean speed and space mean speed.

Time-mean-speed is calculated from the individual speed recorded for vehicles passing a point over a selected time period

\[\begin{aligned} v_t = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N{v_i}}{N} = \frac{50+40+30+20+10}{5} = 30 \ km/h \\ \end{aligned} \]

Space-mean-speed is calculated by dividing the average travel time by the measured distance

\[\begin{aligned} v_s = \frac{N}{\sum_{i=1}^N{\frac{1} {v_i}}} = \frac{5}{1/50+1/40+1/30+1/20+1/10} = 28.9 \ km/h \\ \end{aligned} \]

Relationship between time and space mean speed

Space mean speed weights slower vehicles more heavily, as they take a longer time to occupy a highway section. As such, $v_s$ is typically lower than $v_t$, in which each vehicle is weighted equally.

\[\begin{aligned} v_t = v_s + \frac{\delta^2_s}{v_s} \\ \end{aligned} \]

What is 'density'?

The average number of vehicles that occupy one mile or one kilometer of road space (veh/km)

\[\begin{aligned} Density(k) \quad k = \frac{N}{L} \ (veh/km) \\ \end{aligned} \]

Space occupancy $O_s$: percentage length of highway occupied by vehicles

\[\begin{aligned} Space Occupancy(O_s) \quad O_s = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N{h_i}}{L} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Space headway $\bar{s}$ distance between the same points of two consecutive vehicles following each other

\[\begin{aligned} Spacing(\bar{s}) \quad \bar{s} = \frac{L}{N} = \frac{1}{k} \\ \end{aligned} \]

Travel time and Delay studies

Travel Time: The total time, including stops and delay, to travel from one point to another

Delay: The time difference between the actual travel time and the travel time given no impedances.

Travel time and Delay studies

Type of delay:

Fixed delay is the delay to which a vehicle is subjected regardless of the amount of traffic volume and interference present

Operational delay is the delay caused by interference from other components of the traffic stream.

Travel time delay is the difference between the actual time required to traverse a section of street or highway and the time corresponding to the average speed of traffic at a point of uncongested flow on the section.

Stopped time delay is the time a vehicle is actually standing still because of any influencing factor.

Travel time and Delay studies

Causes of delay:

Fixed delay occurs primarily at intersections. This delay is not a result of the flow characteristics of the traffic stream and could occur with only one vehicle travelling in the section e.g. traffic signals, STOP signs, YIELD signs, railroad crossings.

Operational delay is the result of influences by other traffic. One type of operational delay is caused by other traffic movement that interfere with the stream flow (side frictions), e.g. parking vehicles, turning vehicles, pedestrians, stalled vehicles, double parking, cross traffic, etc, while other type of operational delay is caused by internal frictions within the stream flow, e.g. congestion due to high volume, lack of capacity, merging or weaving manoeuvres.

Travel time data collection: Floating vehicle

A test car attempts to ‘move’ or ‘float’ in the traffic stream.

Travel time data collection: Floating vehicle

The method estimates the average flow over a test section of street / highway as well as the average travel time and average speed in a single operation.

A test vehicle make a series of runs at a reasonable speed consistent with the traffic flow, e.g. attempts to "float" with the traffic, in each direction over the section concerned.

The route should be divided into sections if they are not uniform with respect to physical condition (e.g. width, number of lanes, parking etc.) and traffic conditions (e.g. flow, speed, vehicle types).

Normally, about 3 to 6 runs in each direction under comparable conditions should be averaged for the computations. (The original authors suggested 12 to 16 runs were desirable for acceptable accuracy, but this is seldom necessary or appropriate).

Applicable to road sections with clear visibility of opposing traffic.
